Box Spreads April 5, 2026

Stop Borrowing From Banks.
Your Portfolio Can Do It Better.

Your portfolio is already a bank. You just haven't been using it like one.

When high-income investors need liquidity, most of them do what their advisor told them to do years ago: they call the bank. They take out a line of credit, pledge some assets, pay the rate the bank sets, and move on. It feels routine. It isn't.

What you're actually doing is handing a bank a chunk of your net worth as collateral, letting them decide what it's worth, and paying them a spread on top of whatever rate they feel like charging that quarter. You have no real negotiating power. The terms can change. And if markets move the wrong way, they can call the loan.

There is a better way. It's called a box spread — and most investors have never heard of it, because most advisors can't execute it.

A box spread lets you borrow against your portfolio at near-Treasury rates — with no bank, no credit check, and no one who can change the terms on you.

Here's the mechanics in plain terms. A box spread is a four-leg options position on a broad index. When structured correctly, it creates a perfectly hedged position with a known, fixed payoff at expiration. The difference between what you collect today and what you owe at expiration is, effectively, the interest rate. That rate is set by the market — not by a bank — and it tracks closely to Treasury rates, often coming in well below what any lender would offer you.

You collect the proceeds upfront. You stay fully invested. You don't sell a single share. And when the position expires, you settle the difference. That's the loan. No application. No approval committee. No covenant that lets someone else decide when to pull the plug.

Why does this matter for high earners specifically? Because the tax treatment is favorable. The interest on a box spread is potentially deductible as investment interest expense — a meaningful advantage at California's combined marginal rates. And because you're not selling anything to raise capital, you're not triggering gains. Your appreciated positions keep compounding. Your loss bank stays intact.

Compare that to a traditional line of credit. The interest is often not deductible at all — or only partially. You may have to pledge specific assets. The rate floats. And the psychological weight of having a bank involved in your portfolio decisions is real, even if you never think about it consciously.

The objection I hear most often is: isn't this complicated? The mechanics are. The experience doesn't have to be. At STQ, we implement box spreads through established platforms on the Schwab marketplace. You don't touch the options. You don't manage the legs. You receive the proceeds and we handle the rest.

Your portfolio has been sitting there for years, doing one job. It can do two. The bank knew that. They just didn't tell you.
