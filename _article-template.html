When high-income investors need liquidity, most of them do what their advisor told them years ago: call the bank. Take out a line of credit, pledge some assets, pay whatever rate the bank sets, and move on. It feels routine. It isn't.

What you're actually doing is handing a bank a chunk of your net worth as collateral, letting them decide what it's worth, and paying them a spread on top of whatever rate they feel like charging that quarter. You have no real negotiating power. The terms can change. And if markets move the wrong way, they can call the loan.

There is a better way. It's called a box spread — and most investors have never heard of it, because most advisors can't execute it.

How It Works

A box spread is a four-leg options structure on a broad index. When structured correctly, it creates a perfectly hedged position with a known, fixed payoff at expiration. The difference between what you collect today and what you owe at expiration is, effectively, the interest rate.

That rate is set by the market — not by a bank — and it tracks closely to Treasury rates, often coming in well below what any lender would offer you.

The Rate Advantage
Near-Treasury rates
Set by the market, not a bank. No credit check. No approval committee. No covenant that lets someone else pull the plug.

You collect the proceeds upfront. You stay fully invested. You don't sell a single share. And when the position expires, you settle the difference. That's the loan.

Why It Matters for High Earners

The tax treatment is favorable. The interest on a box spread is potentially deductible as investment interest expense — a meaningful advantage at high marginal tax rates. And because you're not selling anything to raise capital, you're not triggering gains. Your appreciated positions keep compounding. Your loss bank stays intact.

The bank knew your portfolio could do this. They just didn't tell you.

The objection I hear most often is: isn't this complicated? The mechanics are. The experience doesn't have to be.
